Best Parental Control Apps Compared: Bark vs Qustodio vs Net Nanny vs Google Family Link

Built-in parental controls on phones and computers cover the basics, but dedicated parental control apps go further. They monitor content across apps, alert you to concerning conversations, filter the web more aggressively, and give you a single dashboard to manage everything.

The problem? There are dozens of options, and the marketing on every single one makes it sound like the only tool you’ll ever need. After testing four of the most popular options across my own family’s devices over several months, here’s what I actually found.

Bark: Best for Monitoring Teens

What It Does

Bark takes a fundamentally different approach from the other apps on this list. Instead of locking everything down and giving you minute-by-minute usage logs, Bark monitors content across your child’s apps and sends you alerts only when it detects something concerning — cyberbullying, depression, suicidal ideation, sexual content, online predators, violence, or drug-related content.

It monitors over 30 platforms including Instagram, Snapchat, TikTok, YouTube, Gmail, WhatsApp, Discord, and many more. On Android, it can also monitor text messages. The AI scanning is genuinely impressive — it catches concerning context, not just keywords.

What It Costs

  • Bark Jr: $7/month — web filtering and location only, no content monitoring
  • Bark Premium: $14/month or $119/year — full monitoring, web filtering, screen time, and location

Pros

  • Content monitoring is unmatched. No other consumer app monitors as many platforms this effectively. If your teen is being bullied on Discord or encountering predatory behavior on Instagram, Bark is the most likely tool to catch it.
  • Respects teen privacy. You don’t see every message — only flagged ones. This is a selling point for families with older teens who would revolt against full surveillance.
  • Low noise. You’re not drowning in reports. You get an alert when something matters.
  • Covers email and documents. It scans Google Docs and emails, which catches a surprising amount — kids use shared docs as chat rooms.
  • Easy setup. Connect your child’s accounts and install the app. Most monitoring works through account-level access, not device-level.

Cons

  • Screen time management is basic. You can set internet schedules (on/off), but you can’t set per-app time limits. If granular screen time control is important to you, Bark alone won’t cut it.
  • iOS monitoring is limited. Due to Apple’s restrictions, Bark can’t monitor as many apps on iPhone as it can on Android. It relies on connecting to accounts (email, social) rather than on-device scanning.
  • No call/text blocking. Bark monitors texts on Android but can’t block specific contacts.
  • Requires trust in AI. The system decides what’s “concerning.” In my testing, it was accurate the vast majority of the time, but there were occasional false positives and a few missed items.

Verdict

Bark is the best choice for families with tweens and teens who need to monitor social media and messaging without creating a surveillance state. Pair it with built-in device controls (Screen Time on iOS, Family Link on Android) for a complete setup.

Qustodio: Best All-in-One for Multi-Device Families

What It Does

Qustodio aims to be the single dashboard where you manage everything across every device. It covers web filtering, screen time, app controls, location tracking, call and SMS monitoring (Android), YouTube monitoring, and social media activity tracking. It works across iOS, Android, Windows, Mac, Chromebook, and Kindle.

What It Costs

  • Basic: Free — web filtering and screen time for 1 device (very limited)
  • Small Plan: $54.95/year — 5 devices
  • Medium Plan: $96.95/year — 10 devices
  • Large Plan: $137.95/year — 15 devices

Pros

  • True cross-platform management. One dashboard for everything. If your family runs a mix of iPhones, Windows PCs, and a Chromebook, Qustodio handles all of them in one place.
  • Granular screen time. Set per-app time limits, daily device time limits, and scheduled blocks. The daily schedule grid is intuitive — you can block internet access during homework hours while keeping educational apps available.
  • Web filtering is thorough. 29 content categories you can individually allow, block, or set to alert. This is more granular than any built-in control.
  • YouTube monitoring. See what your child searches for and watches on YouTube. This is valuable — YouTube’s algorithm can lead kids to strange places.
  • Call and SMS monitoring on Android. See who your child is communicating with and how often. You can block specific numbers.
  • Panic button. Your child can send an SOS alert with their location to trusted contacts. A genuinely useful safety feature.

Cons

  • iOS limitations are frustrating. Qustodio on iPhone can’t monitor calls, texts, or most social media apps due to Apple’s restrictions. Web filtering on iOS works through a VPN profile, which can occasionally cause connectivity hiccups.
  • The app can be resource-heavy. On older Android phones and Windows machines, Qustodio’s background processes can noticeably affect performance and battery life.
  • Kids can sometimes work around it. Tech-savvy teens have been known to disable the VPN profile on iOS or find workarounds on Android. Qustodio sends you a notification if the app is tampered with, but it’s a cat-and-mouse game.
  • The free plan is almost useless. It’s so limited that it exists mainly to get you to subscribe.
  • Interface feels dated. The parent dashboard works fine, but the design hasn’t kept pace with competitors. It’s functional, not pretty.

Verdict

Qustodio is the best choice for families who want one tool to rule them all, especially those managing many devices across different platforms. It’s strongest on Android and Windows. If your household is all-Apple, the iOS limitations are worth considering.

Net Nanny: Best Web Filtering

What It Does

Net Nanny has been around since the early days of the internet (literally — it launched in 1995), and web filtering remains its core strength. It uses real-time AI analysis to categorize and filter web content as it loads, rather than relying solely on blocklists. This means it catches more and adapts faster. It also includes screen time management, app blocking, location tracking, and activity reporting.

What It Costs

  • 1 Device: $39.99/year
  • 5 Devices: $54.99/year
  • 20 Devices: $89.99/year

Pros

  • Web filtering is the best in class. Net Nanny’s real-time content analysis is noticeably better than competitors at catching inappropriate content on pages that aren’t on any blocklist yet. It analyzes the actual content on the page, not just the URL. This matters on platforms with user-generated content.
  • Profanity masking. Instead of blocking an entire page that contains some profanity, Net Nanny can mask just the profanity. This is useful for older kids researching topics where some results contain mild language.
  • Clean, modern interface. The parent dashboard and app are well-designed and easy to navigate. Setup is straightforward.
  • Flexible content categories. Block, allow, or get warned for categories like Abortion, Gambling, Weapons, Drugs, Pornography, Mature Content, and more. The categories are clear and the filters are accurate.
  • Reasonable pricing. The 5-device plan at $54.99/year is competitive, and the 20-device plan is the best per-device value on this list.

Cons

  • Social media monitoring is limited. Net Nanny doesn’t scan social media content the way Bark does. It can block social media apps or websites, but it won’t alert you to concerning conversations happening within those apps.
  • iOS limitations. Same story as the others — Apple’s restrictions limit what any third-party app can do on iPhone. Web filtering works via a VPN profile, and some apps can bypass it.
  • Screen time controls are good but not the best. You can set daily time limits and schedules, but per-app time limits are less intuitive than Qustodio’s.
  • No call or text monitoring. If monitoring communications is a priority, you’ll need another tool.
  • No free tier. There’s a 14-day trial, but no ongoing free option.

Verdict

Net Nanny is the best choice for families whose primary concern is web content filtering, particularly for elementary and middle school age kids. If you want to make sure your child isn’t stumbling onto inappropriate websites — including obscure or newly created ones — Net Nanny’s real-time analysis is the strongest available. Pair it with built-in device controls for screen time management.

Google Family Link: Best Free Option

What It Does

Google Family Link is a free parental control app that manages Android devices and Chromebooks. It handles app approvals, screen time limits, web filtering, location tracking, and basic activity reporting. It’s built into the Google ecosystem, so there’s nothing extra to install on the child’s device if they’re using a Google account.

What It Costs

Completely free. No premium tiers, no hidden limits.

Pros

  • It’s free. Genuinely, fully free. No features locked behind a paywall. For many families, this alone makes it the right choice.
  • Deep Android integration. Because it’s built by Google, Family Link integrates seamlessly with Android, Chrome, the Play Store, YouTube, and Google Search. There are no VPN profiles to manage and fewer workarounds for kids to exploit.
  • App approval workflow is smooth. When your child tries to install an app, you get a notification with the app’s age rating and a link to its Play Store listing. One tap to approve or deny. It’s fast and easy.
  • Screen time and bedtime controls work reliably. Set a daily limit, set a bedtime, done. The device locks when time’s up. It’s simple and it works.
  • Location tracking is solid. See your child’s device on a map in real time. It uses Google’s location services, which are the most accurate on Android.
  • Activity reports. Weekly email with app usage breakdown. Not as detailed as paid apps, but useful.

Cons

  • Android and Chromebook only. No support for iOS, Windows, or Mac. If your child uses an iPhone or a Windows PC, Family Link can’t help.
  • Web filtering is basic. You can block mature sites and enable SafeSearch, but you can’t customize filtering categories or create granular rules. For young children, the “approved sites only” whitelist mode is effective, but for tweens and teens, it’s too blunt.
  • No content monitoring. Family Link doesn’t scan messages, social media, or app content. It controls what can be installed and how long it can be used, but it has no visibility into what’s happening within apps.
  • Teens can opt out. When a child turns 13 (in most countries), they can choose to remove supervision from their account. Google notifies you, but the child has the power to do it. This is by design — Google treats 13 as the age of digital consent — but it’s frustrating for parents of 13-to-15-year-olds.
  • Limited reporting. You see app usage and web searches, but the detail doesn’t approach what you get from Bark or Qustodio.

Verdict

Google Family Link is the best choice for budget-conscious families using Android devices and for parents of younger children (under 10) where basic controls are sufficient. It does the fundamentals well and costs nothing. As your child grows and their digital world expands, you’ll likely want to supplement or replace it with a paid tool.

Which App Should You Choose?

Here’s my recommendation framework based on what matters most to your family:

Your top concern is monitoring social media and messaging (teens):

Choose Bark. Nothing else comes close for content monitoring. Pair with built-in Screen Time (iOS) or Family Link (Android) for device-level controls.

You want one app to manage everything across many devices:

Choose Qustodio. It’s the most comprehensive all-in-one solution, especially for families with a mix of platforms. Accept the iOS limitations and supplement with Apple’s built-in Screen Time.

Your top concern is web filtering for younger kids:

Choose Net Nanny. Its real-time content analysis is the gold standard for web filtering. Great for the 6-to-12 age range where web browsing is the primary concern.

You need a solid free solution for Android:

Choose Google Family Link. It handles the basics well and the price is right. Perfect for families just starting out with parental controls.

You want the maximum protection:

Layer them. Bark for content monitoring + built-in device controls for screen time + DNS filtering (NextDNS or CleanBrowsing) at the router level. This combination covers the most ground.

Final Thoughts

No single app is perfect, and every family’s needs are different. The best parental control setup is one you’ll actually maintain — so pick the tool that fits your budget, your devices, and your parenting style.

Start with one app, get comfortable with it, and add layers as needed. And remember: the most important parental control is still the conversation you have with your kids about staying safe online.
