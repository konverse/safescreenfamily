The Complete Guide to Parental Controls in 2026 — Every Device, Every Platform

If you’re like me, you’ve stood in your living room and counted the screens. Phone, tablet, laptop, gaming console, smart TV — and that’s just what one kid uses before dinner. The digital world our children navigate is enormous, and setting up parental controls on every single device can feel like a full-time job.

I’ve spent years configuring, testing, and reconfiguring parental controls across our household. This guide is everything I wish someone had handed me on day one: a single, plain-English walkthrough for locking down every major device and platform your family is likely to use in 2026.

Why Parental Controls Still Matter

Let’s get the obvious out of the way. Parental controls are not a substitute for conversation. Talking to your kids about online safety, healthy content, and responsible behavior is always the foundation. But controls give you a safety net — they catch the stuff that slips through when you’re cooking dinner or stuck on a work call.

Modern parental controls can:

  • Filter inappropriate websites and search results
  • Limit screen time and set downtime schedules
  • Block or approve app downloads
  • Monitor location and communication
  • Restrict in-app purchases
  • Set age ratings for games, movies, and TV shows

The key is layering these tools across every device so there are no gaps. Here’s how to do it.

iPhone and iPad (iOS / iPadOS)

Apple’s built-in controls are among the strongest of any platform, and they’ve only gotten better.

Setting Up Screen Time

  1. Open Settings on your child’s device.
  2. Tap Screen Time, then tap Turn On Screen Time.
  3. Choose This is My Child’s Device.
  4. Set Downtime — the hours when only phone calls and apps you allow are available. Most families set this for bedtime through morning (e.g., 8:30 PM to 7:00 AM).
  5. Set App Limits — choose categories like Social Media, Games, or Entertainment and set a daily time allowance.
  6. Under Communication Limits, choose who your child can contact during allowed time and during downtime.
  7. Tap Content & Privacy Restrictions and enable it. This is where the real power is.

Content & Privacy Restrictions Deep Dive

  • App Store Purchases: Require your Apple ID password for every purchase. Disable installing apps entirely if your child is young.
  • Content Restrictions: Set age ratings for Music, Podcasts, Movies, TV Shows, Books, and Apps. Block explicit web content — Apple’s “Limit Adult Websites” filter is solid, though not perfect.
  • Web Content: Choose “Allowed Websites Only” for younger kids (under 8). This creates a whitelist of approved sites.
  • Siri: Disable explicit language and web search content.
  • Location Services: Keep enabled but prevent your child from changing the setting. This lets Find My work.
  • Passcode: Set a Screen Time passcode that is different from the device unlock code. Use something your child won’t guess.

Family Sharing

Set up Family Sharing in Settings so you can manage your child’s account from your own device. You’ll get purchase approval requests, can adjust Screen Time remotely, and share subscriptions without sharing passwords.

Pro tip: If your child is under 13, create a child Apple ID through Family Sharing. This automatically enables age-appropriate defaults and Ask to Buy.

Android Phones and Tablets

Android varies by manufacturer, but Google’s Family Link is the universal tool here and it works well.

Setting Up Google Family Link

  1. Install Google Family Link on your phone (the parent version).
  2. Create a Google Account for your child if they don’t have one, or add supervision to their existing account.
  3. Install Family Link on your child’s device and sign in with their account.
  4. Link the accounts by following the on-screen prompts.

Key Family Link Controls

  • App Approvals: Every app your child tries to download from the Play Store triggers a notification on your phone. You approve or deny.
  • Screen Time Limits: Set a daily limit (e.g., 2 hours) and a bedtime when the device locks. You can set different limits for each day of the week.
  • Content Filters: Set maturity ratings for Google Play apps, movies, TV, books, and music. Restrict explicit content.
  • Google Chrome Filters: Choose “Try to block mature sites” or “Only allow certain sites.” The mature site filter uses Google’s SafeSearch and is reasonably effective.
  • YouTube Settings: Family Link integrates with YouTube. For younger kids, switch them to YouTube Kids entirely. For teens, enable Restricted Mode.
  • Location: See your child’s device location in real time from the Family Link app.

Samsung-Specific Controls

If your child has a Samsung device, you get an extra layer with Samsung Kids. This creates a sandboxed environment with curated apps and content — great for children under 8. Access it from Quick Settings and set a PIN to exit.

Windows 11 PCs and Laptops

Microsoft has built family safety directly into Windows 11, and the 2025/2026 updates have made it significantly better.

Setting Up Microsoft Family Safety

  1. Go to family.microsoft.com and sign in with your Microsoft account.
  2. Add your child as a family member. They’ll need their own Microsoft account (you can create a child account).
  3. On your child’s Windows PC, make sure they sign in with their child account — not yours.
  4. All settings are managed from the Family Safety app (install it on your phone) or the web dashboard.

Key Windows Controls

  • Screen Time: Set daily time allowances and schedules for the Windows PC specifically. You can also set limits for Xbox and mobile from the same dashboard.
  • Web & Search Filters: Microsoft Edge is the enforced browser — content filtering only works in Edge. Enable “Only use allowed websites” for strict whitelisting, or use the age-based filter for broader access. Adult content, violence, and other categories are blocked by default.
  • App & Game Limits: Set age ratings so your child cannot install or run apps and games above their maturity level. Set per-app time limits for specific games.
  • Activity Reports: Get weekly email summaries of your child’s web browsing, app usage, search queries, and screen time.
  • Spend: Manage your child’s Microsoft account balance and require approval for purchases.

Important note: These controls only apply when your child is signed into their own Microsoft account on the PC. If they can access an admin or guest account, the controls are bypassed. Make sure other accounts on the PC are password-protected.

Windows Local Account Restrictions

For an additional layer, you can set your child’s Windows account as a Standard (non-administrator) account. This prevents them from installing desktop applications, changing system settings, or disabling security features.

Mac (macOS)

Apple’s approach on Mac mirrors iOS closely, making it easier if you’re already an Apple household.

Setting Up Screen Time on Mac

  1. Open System Settings > Screen Time.
  2. If your child has their own Mac user account (they should), sign in with their child Apple ID.
  3. Enable Screen Time and configure from your parent device via Family Sharing, or directly on the Mac.

Key Mac Controls

  • App Limits: Set daily time limits per app or per category.
  • Downtime: Schedule hours when the Mac is locked except for allowed apps.
  • Content & Privacy: Restrict web content in Safari (Limit Adult Websites or Allowed Websites Only). Set age ratings for the App Store, movies, and music.
  • Communication Limits: Control who your child can communicate with via Messages, FaceTime, and Phone.
  • Always Allowed: Whitelist specific apps that remain available during downtime (homework tools, for example).

Pro tip: Combine macOS Screen Time with a DNS-level filter like CleanBrowsing or NextDNS for more robust web filtering that covers all browsers, not just Safari.

Chromebook

Chromebooks are popular in schools, and Google’s tight integration makes parental controls straightforward.

Setting Up a Supervised Chromebook

  1. Your child needs a supervised Google Account (set up via Family Link — see the Android section above).
  2. Sign your child into the Chromebook with their supervised account.
  3. Manage all settings from the Family Link app on your phone.

Key Chromebook Controls

  • SafeSearch: Enforced by default on supervised accounts. Cannot be turned off by the child.
  • Website Restrictions: Same Chrome filters as Android — block mature sites or whitelist specific ones.
  • App Management: Control which apps and extensions your child can install from the Chrome Web Store.
  • Screen Time: Set daily limits and bedtime. The Chromebook locks when time is up.
  • Permissions: Manage camera, microphone, location, and notification permissions on a per-site basis.

School-issued Chromebooks: If the school manages the device, your parental controls may conflict with the school’s admin policies. Check with the school’s IT department about what additional controls you can layer on.

PlayStation 5 (PS5)

Sony has a comprehensive parental controls system, but it’s not always intuitive to find.

Setting Up PS5 Parental Controls

  1. On the PS5, go to Settings > Family and Parental Controls > Family Management.
  2. Create a child account linked to your adult PlayStation Network account.
  3. Set the child’s age correctly — this determines default restrictions.

Key PS5 Controls

  • Age Rating Restrictions: Set the maximum age rating for games (e.g., PEGI or ESRB). The PS5 will block games above this level.
  • Communication Restrictions: Disable or restrict chat, messaging, user-generated content, and voice chat. This is critical for online multiplayer.
  • Spending Limits: Set a monthly spending limit on the PlayStation Store.
  • Play Time: Set a daily play time duration. You can choose whether the PS5 warns or automatically logs the child out when time expires.
  • Web Browser: The PS5’s browser is limited, but you can disable it entirely.
  • Privacy Settings: Restrict who can see your child’s online status, profile, and friend list.

Pro tip: Use the PlayStation Family App on your phone to manage these settings remotely and get play time notifications.

Xbox Series X/S

Microsoft’s family settings for Xbox are managed through the same Microsoft Family Safety system as Windows — a real advantage if your family uses both.

Setting Up Xbox Parental Controls

  1. Go to family.microsoft.com or use the Xbox Family Settings app on your phone.
  2. Add your child to your Microsoft family group (same as for Windows).
  3. On the Xbox, sign your child in with their child Microsoft account.

Key Xbox Controls

  • Content Restrictions: Set the maximum age rating for games and media. Block specific games by title.
  • Screen Time: Set daily time limits and schedules — same dashboard as Windows.
  • Social Controls: Manage who your child can communicate with, add as friends, and play multiplayer with. Options range from “no one” to “friends only” to “everyone.”
  • Purchases: Require parent approval for all purchases and downloads.
  • Activity Reports: See what games your child played and for how long.

Pro tip: The Xbox Family Settings phone app is excellent. You can approve requests, adjust time limits, and see activity all from your pocket.

Nintendo Switch

Nintendo’s parental controls are solid and managed through a dedicated phone app.

Setting Up Switch Parental Controls

  1. Download the Nintendo Switch Parental Controls app on your phone.
  2. Open the app, create or sign into your Nintendo Account.
  3. On the Switch, go to System Settings > Parental Controls > Use Your Smart Device. A registration code will appear.
  4. Enter the code in the phone app to link the devices.

Key Switch Controls

  • Play Time Limit: Set a daily play time limit. When time’s up, an alarm notification appears on the Switch. You can choose to also suspend the software automatically.
  • Restriction Level: Choose a preset (Child, Pre-Teen, Teen) or customize. Presets restrict game age ratings, social features, and communication.
  • Online Communication: Restrict or disable the ability to post to social media and communicate with other players.
  • eShop Restrictions: Require a PIN to access the Nintendo eShop.
  • Monthly Summary: The app sends a monthly play activity report showing what games were played and for how long.

Heads up: The Switch’s web browser is hidden and limited, but some games include in-game browsers. Content filtering on the Switch is less granular than on PlayStation or Xbox. For younger children, stick to the “Child” restriction level.

Smart TVs (Samsung, LG, Roku, Fire TV, Apple TV)

Smart TVs often fly under the parental controls radar, but every major streaming app on them can be locked down.

General Smart TV Strategy

The most effective approach is to set parental controls within each streaming app rather than relying on TV-level controls, which are inconsistent across brands.

Netflix

  1. Go to Account > Profile & Parental Controls on the Netflix website.
  2. Select your child’s profile.
  3. Set Maturity Rating to the appropriate level (e.g., PG for young kids, PG-13 for tweens).
  4. Set a Profile Lock PIN on your adult profile so your child can’t switch to it.
  5. Disable Auto-Play on the child’s profile if you want more control.

Disney+

  1. Go to Profile > Edit Profiles > select the child’s profile.
  2. Toggle Kids Profile on. This limits content to kid-friendly titles only.
  3. Set a Content Rating if you want the child to access some PG-13 content.
  4. Set a Profile PIN on adult profiles.

YouTube (on Smart TV)

  1. Use the YouTube Kids app instead of regular YouTube for children under 10.
  2. For older kids, sign them into their supervised Google Account and enable Restricted Mode.
  3. Restricted Mode can be locked on from the YouTube settings menu on the TV.

Amazon Fire TV / Fire Stick

  1. Go to Settings > Preferences > Parental Controls.
  2. Enable Parental Controls and set a PIN.
  3. This PIN will be required to make purchases, open apps, and play content above your chosen rating.
  4. For younger kids, enable Amazon Kids (formerly FreeTime), which creates a walled-garden experience with curated content.

Roku

  1. Go to Settings > Parental Controls.
  2. Set a PIN.
  3. Enable restrictions for purchasing and adding channels.
  4. Set movie and TV rating limits.
  5. Note: Roku’s built-in controls are limited. You’ll still need to configure restrictions within individual apps.

Apple TV

  1. Go to Settings > General > Restrictions.
  2. Enable Restrictions and set a passcode.
  3. Set age ratings for Movies, TV Shows, Apps, and Music.
  4. Restrict the ability to install or delete apps.
  5. If using Family Sharing, the child’s Apple TV profile inherits Screen Time settings.

Network-Level Protection: The Layer Most Parents Miss

Device-level controls are essential, but network-level filtering adds a powerful safety layer that covers every device on your home network — including smart devices, guest devices, and anything your kid’s friend brings over.

DNS Filtering

Change your home router’s DNS settings to a family-safe DNS provider:

  • CleanBrowsing Family Filter: 185.228.168.168 — blocks adult content, malware, and mixed content.
  • OpenDNS FamilyShield: 208.67.222.123 — similar protection, run by Cisco.
  • NextDNS: Offers granular control via a dashboard. You can block specific categories, set time-based rules, and see query logs. This is the most customizable option.

To apply this, log into your router’s admin panel (usually 192.168.1.1 or 192.168.0.1), find the DNS settings, and replace the existing DNS addresses with your chosen provider.

Router-Level Controls

Many modern routers (Eero, Netgear Armor, TP-Link HomeCare) offer built-in parental controls including content filtering, device-level time limits, and usage reports. Check your router’s app or admin panel.

Putting It All Together: A Practical Checklist

Here’s a quick-reference checklist for locking down your household:

  • Set up Family Sharing / Family Link — centralized management for Apple or Google ecosystems.
  • Create child accounts on every platform — never let kids use your adult accounts.
  • Configure Screen Time / time limits on phones, tablets, computers, and consoles.
  • Set content/age ratings on every device and every streaming app.
  • Enable purchase approvals everywhere.
  • Restrict communication on gaming consoles and social apps.
  • Set up DNS filtering on your home router.
  • Lock your own profiles with PINs on shared devices like TVs.
  • Review settings quarterly — as kids grow, their needs (and their cleverness) change.

A Word About Trust and Balance

I want to end on this note because it matters. Parental controls are guardrails, not prison walls. The goal is to create an age-appropriate digital environment while gradually giving your kids more freedom as they demonstrate responsibility.

Every few months, sit down with your child and review the controls together. Explain why certain limits exist. Let them earn expanded access. This turns a potential source of conflict into a conversation about responsibility and trust.

No tool is a substitute for that conversation. But with the controls in this guide configured, you’ll have a solid safety net while you have it.
