TikTok Parental Controls: How to Keep Your Kids Safe

April 1, 2026 · 9 min read

TikTok. The app that’s simultaneously your kid’s favorite thing in the world and your biggest source of parental anxiety. Whether you love it, hate it, or are still trying to figure out what a “FYP” is, the reality is that TikTok is probably already on your child’s phone – or they’re begging for it.

Rather than fighting a losing battle against it entirely, let’s focus on something practical: making it as safe as possible. TikTok actually has a pretty robust set of parental controls, and most parents don’t know about half of them.

Here’s every setting you need to know about, and exactly how to configure them.

First: Should Your Kid Even Be on TikTok?

Let’s address this before we get into settings.

TikTok’s official minimum age is 13. If your child is under 13, they shouldn’t be on TikTok. Full stop. The platform isn’t designed for younger kids, the algorithm doesn’t adequately filter content for them, and it violates TikTok’s terms of service.

For ages 13-15, TikTok applies automatic restrictions (more on those below), but parental oversight is still important.

For ages 16+, the automatic restrictions loosen, but you can still apply Family Pairing to maintain some guardrails.

Now, if your child is 13+ and you’ve decided to allow TikTok, here’s how to lock it down properly.

Setting Up Family Pairing (The Most Important Step)

Family Pairing is TikTok’s built-in parental control system. It links your TikTok account to your child’s, giving you control over key settings from your own device.

How to set it up:

On your phone:

  1. Download TikTok and create an account (you don’t have to post anything or use it otherwise)
  2. Go to Profile > Menu (three lines) > Settings and Privacy
  3. Tap Family Pairing
  4. Select Parent
  5. A QR code will appear

On your child’s phone:

  1. Go to Profile > Menu > Settings and Privacy
  2. Tap Family Pairing
  3. Select Teen
  4. Scan the QR code from your phone

That’s it. You’re now linked. Changes you make in Family Pairing on your phone will apply to your child’s account.

Essential Settings to Configure

1. Screen Time Management

TikTok’s screen time controls have gotten much better. Here’s what to set:

Daily screen time limit:

  1. In Family Pairing, tap Screen Time
  2. Set a daily time limit (TikTok now defaults to 60 minutes for accounts under 18)
  3. Set the passcode (your child will need this to request more time)

When the limit is reached, TikTok shows a screen time notice. Your child can enter the passcode you set to get more time, or send you a request.

Scheduled screen time breaks: You can also set specific times when TikTok is unavailable:

  1. Tap Screen Time > Schedule
  2. Block times when you don’t want TikTok active (bedtime hours, school hours, etc.)

What I recommend for different ages:

  • Ages 13-14: 45-60 minutes daily, blocked from 9 PM to 7 AM
  • Ages 15-16: 60-90 minutes daily, blocked from 10 PM to 6:30 AM
  • Ages 17+: Consider letting them self-manage with the built-in reminders

2. Restricted Mode

Restricted Mode limits the appearance of content that may not be appropriate for all audiences. It’s not perfect – no filter is – but it meaningfully reduces exposure to mature content.

  1. In Family Pairing, tap Restricted Mode
  2. Toggle it On

What Restricted Mode filters:

  • Content with mature or suggestive themes
  • Graphic or shocking material
  • Content discussing drugs or alcohol
  • Some content with profanity

What it doesn’t catch: subtle manipulation, unrealistic body standards, dangerous challenges framed as “fun,” and emotionally harmful content that doesn’t technically violate guidelines. This is why settings alone aren’t enough – you also need conversations.

3. Direct Messages (DMs)

This is critical. Control who can send your child private messages:

  1. In Family Pairing, find Direct Messages
  2. Options:
    • Off – nobody can DM your child (safest for younger teens)
    • Friends only – only people your child has mutually followed
    • Everyone – do not use this for minors

My recommendation: Set to “Off” for ages 13-14. Set to “Friends only” for 15+. There’s no good reason for a stranger to DM your child on TikTok.

For accounts under 16, TikTok already restricts DMs by default. But verify it’s actually set correctly – default settings can sometimes be changed.

4. Comments

Control who can comment on your child’s videos:

  1. In Privacy settings, find Comments
  2. Options: Everyone, Friends, or Off

Set to “Friends” for younger teens. Open comments from strangers can include harassment, inappropriate remarks, and predatory behavior. If your kid doesn’t post publicly, this is less of a concern.

5. Who Can View and Interact

Configure these privacy settings:

  • Private account: Toggle on. This means only approved followers can see your child’s content. For kids under 16, TikTok defaults to private.
  • Suggest account to others: Toggle off. This prevents TikTok from recommending your child’s profile to strangers.
  • Allow downloads: Toggle off. This prevents people from downloading your child’s videos and sharing them elsewhere.
  • Allow Stitch and Duet: Toggle off for younger teens. These features let strangers use your child’s video in their own content.

6. Search Visibility

  1. Go to Privacy > Discoverability
  2. Toggle off Allow search engines to show your profile

This prevents your child’s TikTok profile from appearing in Google search results.

7. Content Preferences

TikTok now offers keyword filtering:

  1. Go to Content Preferences > Filter Keywords
  2. Add words or hashtags you want filtered from your child’s For You page and Following feed
  3. Consider adding terms related to:
    • Self-harm and eating disorders
    • Dangerous challenges
    • Drug-related slang
    • Any specific topics you’re concerned about

This won’t catch everything (people misspell words intentionally to bypass filters), but it helps.

Understanding TikTok’s Automatic Protections for Teens

TikTok applies some automatic restrictions based on age. It’s worth knowing what’s already in place:

For accounts under 16:

  • Account is private by default
  • DMs are turned off
  • Videos cannot be downloaded by others
  • Going “Live” is not available
  • Push notifications are disabled after 9 PM

For accounts 16-17:

  • DMs are limited to friends
  • Going Live is available
  • Push notifications are disabled after 10 PM
  • Duet and Stitch are available but restricted by default

Important caveat: These protections only work if your child entered their real birthday when creating their account. If they lied about their age (which is extremely common), they get adult-level defaults. Check their account settings to verify.

The Algorithm: The Risk You Can’t Fully Control

TikTok’s recommendation algorithm is incredibly powerful – and that’s the problem. It learns what your child engages with and serves more of it. If they watch a video about feeling sad, they’ll get more sad content. If they watch content about weight loss, they’ll get more of it. This can create negative feedback loops that are genuinely harmful.

What you can do:

Reset the algorithm periodically. TikTok now allows you to reset the For You feed recommendations:

  1. Go to Settings > Content Preferences > Refresh Your For You Feed
  2. This clears the algorithm’s profile and starts fresh

Teach your child to train the algorithm. Show them how to:

  • Long-press on a video and select “Not interested” for content they don’t want more of
  • Actively search for and engage with positive content they enjoy
  • Scroll past (don’t watch) content that makes them feel bad

Monitor mood changes. If your child’s mood notably shifts after TikTok use – more anxious, more self-critical, more withdrawn – the algorithm may be serving them harmful content. That’s a signal to reset the feed and have a conversation.

Common TikTok Dangers and How to Address Each One

Dangerous Challenges

TikTok challenges go viral fast, and some are genuinely dangerous. You can’t block every challenge, but you can:

  • Talk to your child about evaluating challenges critically (“Would I do this if no one was filming?”)
  • Follow news about current trending challenges
  • Make it clear that no video is worth getting hurt

Body Image and Self-Esteem

The beauty-filtered, curated reality of TikTok can seriously impact self-esteem, especially for girls. Counter this by:

  • Talking openly about filters and editing
  • Following body-positive creators together
  • Monitoring if your child becomes overly focused on appearance after TikTok use

Scams and Phishing

Teens are targeted by scams – fake giveaways, phishing links in comments, “modeling agency” DMs. Teach your child:

  • Never click links from strangers
  • No legitimate company recruits through TikTok DMs
  • If it sounds too good to be true, it is

Predatory Behavior

Predators use TikTok to identify and contact minors. The best defenses are:

  • Private account (enabled by default under 16)
  • DMs off or friends-only
  • Never sharing personal information in videos (school uniform, street signs, bedroom details that reveal location)
  • Knowing the warning signs: excessive compliments, requests to move to another platform, asking to keep the relationship secret

Having “The TikTok Talk” with Your Kid

The settings above create a safety net, but the real protection comes from your child understanding why these precautions exist. Some conversation starters:

“Show me your favorite TikTok accounts.” This opens the door to understanding what they’re consuming without being invasive.

“What would you do if someone you didn’t know DM’d you something weird?” Practice the response together.

“Do you ever see stuff on TikTok that makes you feel bad about yourself?” Ask directly. Many kids won’t bring this up on their own but will answer honestly when asked.

“Let’s set these safety settings up together.” Collaboration is better than imposition. They’ll understand the settings, feel respected, and be less motivated to circumvent them.

When to Consider Removing TikTok

Sometimes the right answer is no TikTok, at least temporarily. Consider it if:

  • Your child is under 13
  • Their mood, sleep, or behavior has notably worsened since joining TikTok
  • They’re unable to self-regulate their usage even with screen time limits
  • They’ve had a safety incident (predatory contact, cyberbullying, etc.) and need a reset
  • TikTok is interfering with school, friendships, or family relationships

Removing TikTok doesn’t have to be permanent or punitive. Frame it as a break: “Let’s take a month off and see how you feel.” Often, kids are relieved once the initial resistance passes.
