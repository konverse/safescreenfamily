Parental Controls for Gaming: PS5, Xbox, Switch & PC Complete Guide

April 1, 2026 · 12 min read

Your kid games. Probably a lot. And while gaming itself isn’t the problem – it can be genuinely beneficial for coordination, problem-solving, and social connection – the online components of modern gaming come with real risks: voice chat with strangers, in-game purchases that add up fast, and content that ranges from mild to extremely violent.

The good news is that every major gaming platform has parental controls built in. The bad news is that they’re all in different places, have different names, and none of them are turned on by default.

This guide covers the four major platforms: PlayStation 5, Xbox (Series X/S), Nintendo Switch, and PC. For each one, I’ll walk you through the settings that actually matter and exactly how to configure them.

Let’s get your kid’s gaming setup dialed in.

PlayStation 5 (PS5)

Sony’s parental controls are managed through the PlayStation Family Management system. You’ll need your own PSN account as the “family manager.”

Initial Setup

  1. On the PS5, go to Settings > Family and Parental Controls > Family Management
  2. Sign in with your PSN account (this becomes the family manager)
  3. Select Add Family Member > Add a Child
  4. Create a PSN account for your child with their real date of birth
  5. Set a spending limit (more on this below)

Key Settings to Configure

Age Level for Games

  1. Go to Family Management > [Child’s Account] > Parental Controls
  2. Under Age Level for Games, set the appropriate content rating

The levels correspond to ESRB ratings:

  • Level 1-2: EC (Early Childhood) and E (Everyone) – ages 3-6
  • Level 3-5: E10+ and T (Teen) – ages 7-12
  • Level 6-7: M (Mature) – ages 13-16
  • Level 9: Allows all content – only for 17+

Set this to match your child’s actual age or the content level you’re comfortable with. If your 12-year-old wants to play a T-rated game, that’s your call. But this prevents them from accessing M-rated games without your knowledge.

Communication and User-Generated Content

  1. In Parental Controls, find Communication and User-Generated Content
  2. Options:
    • Not Allowed: Your child can’t send or receive messages, use voice chat, or see user-generated content. Safest option for younger kids.
    • Restricted: Can communicate with friends only.
    • Allowed: No restrictions.

My recommendation: “Restricted” for kids under 14. “Allowed” for older teens who’ve demonstrated good judgment. Voice chat with random strangers in online games is where most of the toxic behavior happens.

Spending Limits

  1. In Family Management, find Monthly Spending Limit
  2. Set to $0 if you want to approve every purchase
  3. Or set a reasonable monthly allowance

Important: Also go to Settings > Users and Accounts > Account > Payment and Subscriptions and make sure “Require Password at Checkout” is enabled. This prevents impulse purchases even if your child has access to the store.

Play Time Controls

  1. In Parental Controls, select Play Time Settings
  2. Set the Time Zone and Restricted Play Time
  3. Choose what happens when time runs out:
    • Notify Only: Shows a message but doesn’t force them to stop
    • Log Out: Actually ends the session

For younger kids (under 12), use “Log Out.” For teens, “Notify Only” gives them responsibility while keeping you informed.

Online Play

You can restrict whether your child can play online at all, or limit it to friends only. For young kids who primarily play single-player games, turning off online play entirely is the simplest solution.

PS5 Pro Tips

  • Check the monthly play time report in Family Management. It shows exactly what games they played and for how long.
  • PlayStation’s parental controls app lets you manage everything from your phone, including approving friend requests and purchase requests.
  • Game-specific settings matter too. Games like Fortnite and Roblox have their own parental controls within the game, separate from PlayStation’s. Configure both.

Xbox (Series X/S)

Microsoft’s parental controls are managed through the Xbox Family Settings app or the Microsoft Family Safety system. They’re actually some of the most comprehensive across any platform.

Initial Setup

  1. Download the Xbox Family Settings app on your phone
  2. Sign in with your Microsoft account
  3. Add your child by creating a child Microsoft account or linking an existing one
  4. Your child’s Xbox will automatically apply the settings from your family group

Key Settings to Configure

Content Restrictions

  1. In the Family Settings app, select your child
  2. Tap Content Restrictions
  3. Set the age rating limit for games, apps, movies, and TV

Xbox uses the same ESRB ratings as PlayStation. Set the limit that matches your family’s comfort level.

Screen Time

  1. In Family Settings, tap Screen Time
  2. Set daily time limits for each day of the week (weekdays and weekends can be different)
  3. Set allowed hours (e.g., gaming allowed from 3 PM to 8 PM on school days)

Xbox will notify your child 30 minutes and 15 minutes before their time runs out. When time expires, the console locks. Your child can request more time, and you approve or deny from your phone.

This is one of Xbox’s best features. The app-based approval system is quick and painless. Your kid hits their limit, sends a request, you get a notification, and you can grant 30 more minutes with a single tap (or not).

Communication and Multiplayer

  1. In Family Settings, go to Privacy and Online Safety
  2. Key options:
    • Others can communicate with voice, text, or invites: Set to “Friends” or “Block”
    • Others can see your Xbox profile: Set to “Friends” or “Block”
    • You can join multiplayer games: Allow or Block
    • You can add friends: Allow (but you can require approval for each friend request)

For kids under 13, I recommend blocking communication with non-friends and requiring approval for friend requests.

Spending

  1. In Family Settings, go to Spending
  2. Set a spending limit or require approval for all purchases
  3. Add money to your child’s account manually rather than linking a credit card

Never link your credit card directly to a child’s account. Add funds in specific amounts. This creates a natural spending limit and prevents surprise charges.

Activity Reports

Xbox sends weekly activity reports to your email showing:

  • What games were played
  • How long each was played
  • Online activity
  • Spending

Actually read these. It takes 2 minutes and gives you a clear picture of your child’s gaming habits without hovering over their shoulder.

Xbox Pro Tips

  • The Xbox Family Settings app is genuinely excellent. You can manage almost everything from your phone.
  • Xbox’s “Ask a Parent” feature lets your child request access to blocked content. You get a notification with details and can approve or deny instantly.
  • For Minecraft specifically (owned by Microsoft), there are additional safety settings at account.xbox.com under the child’s profile.

Nintendo Switch

Nintendo’s approach is family-friendlier by default – most Switch games are aimed at younger audiences. But the console does have online play, a chat function, and an eShop, so parental controls still matter.

Initial Setup

  1. Download the Nintendo Switch Parental Controls app on your phone
  2. Create a Nintendo Account (or sign in)
  3. The app will display a registration code
  4. On the Switch, go to System Settings > Parental Controls > Use Your Smart Device
  5. Enter the registration code

Key Settings to Configure

Restriction Level

The app offers preset restriction levels:

  • Young child: Restricts to games rated for young children. Blocks posting on social media, communicating with others, and VR mode.
  • Child: Allows games up to E10+. Same communication restrictions.
  • Pre-teen: Allows games up to T (Teen). Communication still restricted.
  • Teen: Allows all content except the most mature. Communication allowed.
  • Custom: Pick and choose individual settings.

For most families, the preset that matches your child’s age group is a good starting point. Use Custom if you want more control.

Play Time Limit

  1. In the Parental Controls app, tap Play Time Limit
  2. Set the daily limit (30 minutes to 6 hours, in 15-minute increments)
  3. Choose what happens at the limit:
    • Alarm Only: The Switch displays a notification but gaming continues
    • Suspend Software: The game actually pauses and the system goes to sleep

For younger kids, use Suspend Software. The alarm-only option is easily ignored.

You can also set a Bedtime Alarm – a specific time when the Switch reminds your child to stop playing.

Restriction on Communicating with Others

This blocks your child from sending and receiving messages, images, and friend requests. For kids under 12, turn this on.

Restriction on Posting to Social Media

The Switch can post screenshots and videos to social media. For younger kids, restrict this.

eShop Restrictions

You can require a password for all eShop purchases, or restrict eShop access entirely for younger children.

Switch Pro Tips

  • The Parental Controls app sends you a daily play time summary showing what games were played and for how long. Check it regularly.
  • Nintendo Switch Online (the paid subscription for online play) has its own parental controls for voice chat through the Nintendo Switch Online phone app. If your child uses this for games like Splatoon, configure it separately.
  • The Switch doesn’t have a web browser (by design), which eliminates one common risk entirely.
  • For Fortnite on Switch: The game has its own parental controls (Epic Games account settings) that are separate from the Switch’s. You need to configure both.

PC Gaming

PC is the trickiest platform because there’s no single walled garden. Games come from Steam, Epic Games, Battle.net, and dozens of other sources, each with their own settings. Plus, the PC itself has full internet access.

Layer 1: Operating System Level

Windows Family Safety

  1. Go to Settings > Accounts > Family
  2. Add your child’s Microsoft account as a family member
  3. Configure settings at family.microsoft.com

Key settings:

  • Screen Time: Set daily limits and allowed hours specifically for the PC
  • Content Filters: Block apps and games by age rating
  • Web Browsing: Filter results and block specific sites (works in Edge; consider using Edge as their default)
  • Spending: Control Microsoft Store purchases
  • Activity Reporting: Get weekly emails about PC usage

macOS Screen Time

If your child games on a Mac:

  1. Go to System Settings > Screen Time
  2. Enable it for your child’s account
  3. Configure content restrictions, app limits, and downtime (similar to iPhone)

Layer 2: Steam Parental Controls

Steam is the most popular PC game platform, and its parental controls are called Steam Family View:

  1. Open Steam and go to Steam > Settings > Family
  2. Click Manage Family View
  3. Choose which features your child can access:
    • Store (restrict or allow)
    • Community features (forums, chat, user profiles)
    • Friends list and chat
    • Specific game categories by content rating
  4. Set a PIN that locks/unlocks the restricted features

Steam Family View is decent but not perfect. A tech-savvy teenager can potentially find workarounds. Pair it with OS-level controls.

Steam Purchase Controls

  • Enable Steam Guard two-factor authentication on the account
  • Don’t store payment information on your child’s Steam account
  • Use Steam Family Sharing to share your game library with your child’s separate account (you control what’s shared)
  • Consider using Steam Gift Cards with specific amounts rather than linking a credit card

Layer 3: Game-Specific Settings

Major online games have their own safety settings:

Fortnite (Epic Games):

  • Go to epicgames.com and log into your child’s account
  • Enable Parental Controls (requires a PIN)
  • Configure: voice chat, text chat, friend requests, mature content filtering

Roblox:

  • Go to roblox.com and log into your child’s account
  • Settings > Privacy and Security
  • Configure contact and chat settings
  • Enable Account Restrictions for the strictest filtering
  • Set a PIN to prevent changes

Minecraft:

  • Managed through Xbox/Microsoft Family Safety settings
  • In-game: Settings > Multiplayer settings

Layer 4: Router-Level Controls (Optional but Powerful)

For the most comprehensive approach, configure parental controls at the router level:

  • Many modern routers (Netgear, Asus, etc.) have built-in parental controls
  • You can set time schedules for specific devices (the gaming PC only gets internet from 3-8 PM)
  • Block specific websites at the network level
  • Monitor data usage

This works regardless of what platform or game your child uses, because all traffic goes through the router.

PC Pro Tips

  • Keep the gaming PC in a common area. This isn’t surveillance – it’s natural accountability. Kids behave differently when their screen is visible.
  • For Discord (which most PC gamers use for voice chat), see our separate Discord safety guide.
  • Game mods (modifications) can add content that isn’t in the base game, including explicit content. Be aware if your child installs mods.
  • Free-to-play games make money through microtransactions. These can add up to hundreds of dollars if unchecked. Set spending controls before they start playing any free-to-play game.

Cross-Platform Tips That Apply Everywhere

Talk About Online Interactions

Across all platforms, the biggest risk isn’t the games themselves – it’s the people your child interacts with online. Have clear rules:

  • Never share personal information (real name, school, location, age)
  • Never accept friend requests from people they don’t know in real life
  • If someone makes them uncomfortable, block and tell a parent
  • Voice chat with strangers is a privilege, not a right – and it can be revoked

Manage In-Game Spending

The gaming industry has perfected the art of getting kids to spend money. Loot boxes, battle passes, skins, virtual currency – it’s designed to be irresistible.

Set a clear monthly gaming budget. Give your child that amount (through gift cards, not credit cards) and let them decide how to spend it. When it’s gone, it’s gone. This teaches budgeting while preventing surprise charges.

Check Age Ratings Before They Play

The ESRB ratings exist for a reason:

  • E (Everyone): Safe for all ages
  • E10+ (Everyone 10+): Mild cartoon violence, minimal suggestive themes
  • T (Teen): Violence, some blood, crude humor, minimal suggestive themes
  • M (Mature 17+): Intense violence, blood/gore, sexual content, strong language

Use esrb.org to look up any game and see detailed content descriptions. The summaries tell you exactly what type of content to expect.

Watch for Warning Signs

Keep an eye out for:

  • Dramatic mood changes after gaming (especially after online interactions)
  • Secrecy about who they’re playing with
  • Requests for money or gift cards outside normal patterns
  • Staying up extremely late to play
  • Declining school performance or abandoning other activities

These don’t necessarily mean something bad is happening, but they’re worth a conversation.
