How to Set Up iPhone Parental Controls: Step-by-Step Guide for 2026

April 1, 2026 · 9 min read

So your kid has an iPhone (or is about to get one), and you want to make sure they’re safe without turning the thing into an expensive paperweight. Good news: Apple’s built-in parental controls are actually pretty solid in 2026 – if you know where to find everything and how to set it up properly.

This guide walks you through every setting that matters, step by step. No tech background required. If you can send a text message, you can do this.

Before You Start: What You’ll Need

  • Your own iPhone or iPad (with iOS 18 or later)
  • Your child’s iPhone (with iOS 18 or later)
  • An Apple ID for your child (you can create one during setup)
  • About 20-30 minutes for the initial setup
  • A conversation with your kid about why you’re doing this (more on that at the end)

Step 1: Set Up Family Sharing

Family Sharing is the foundation. It connects your Apple ID to your child’s, which lets you manage their settings remotely from your own device.

  1. Open Settings on your iPhone
  2. Tap your name at the top
  3. Tap Family Sharing
  4. Tap Add Member
  5. Choose Create an Account for a Child if your kid doesn’t have an Apple ID yet
  6. Follow the prompts to enter their name, birthday, and create their Apple ID

Important: Make sure you enter their real birthday. Apple uses this to automatically apply age-appropriate defaults. If you say your 8-year-old is 16, they’ll get 16-year-old settings.

If your child already has an Apple ID, you can invite them to your family group instead. They’ll need to accept the invitation.

Step 2: Enable Screen Time

Screen Time is Apple’s hub for all parental controls. Here’s how to turn it on and configure it:

On your child’s device:

  1. Go to Settings > Screen Time
  2. Tap Turn On Screen Time
  3. Tap This is My Child’s iPhone
  4. Set a Screen Time Passcode (use something your kid won’t guess – not their birthday or 1234)

Or remotely from your device:

  1. Go to Settings > Family > [Child’s Name]
  2. Tap Screen Time
  3. Toggle it on and set a passcode

Pro tip: Write down the Screen Time passcode somewhere safe. If you forget it, resetting it is a headache.

Step 3: Set Downtime

Downtime is a scheduled period where only apps you specifically allow (and phone calls) will work. This is your best tool for managing nighttime phone use.

  1. In Screen Time, tap Downtime
  2. Toggle it on
  3. Set a schedule – most parents do something like 9:00 PM to 7:00 AM on school nights

During Downtime, your child can request more time for specific apps. You’ll get a notification on your phone and can approve or deny it remotely.

What I recommend: Set Downtime to start 30 minutes before your kid’s actual bedtime. This gives them a wind-down buffer and prevents the “just five more minutes” battle.

Step 4: Set App Limits

App Limits let you cap how long your child spends in specific apps or categories of apps per day.

  1. In Screen Time, tap App Limits
  2. Tap Add Limit
  3. Choose a category (Social, Games, Entertainment, etc.) or specific apps
  4. Set the time allowed per day
  5. Tap Add

Recommended starting limits (adjust based on age and your family values):

CategoryAges 6-9Ages 10-12Ages 13-15
Games30-60 min60 min90 min
Social MediaNot applicable30 min60 min
Entertainment (YouTube, etc.)30 min60 min90 min
EducationalUnlimitedUnlimitedUnlimited

These are starting points, not commandments. Every family is different, and what matters most is that you’ve thought about it intentionally rather than defaulting to unlimited.

Important setting: Make sure “Block at End of Limit” is toggled on. Otherwise, the limit is just a notification that your kid can dismiss and keep using the app.

Step 5: Configure Content & Privacy Restrictions

This is the big one. Content & Privacy Restrictions control what your child can access, install, and see.

  1. In Screen Time, tap Content & Privacy Restrictions
  2. Toggle it on

Now go through each section:

App Store & Purchases

  • Installing Apps: Set to “Allow” but turn on Ask to Buy (this means any purchase or free download requires your approval)
  • Deleting Apps: Your call. Some parents prevent deletion so monitoring apps stay installed
  • In-App Purchases: Set to “Don’t Allow” unless you want surprise charges

Content Restrictions

This controls what kind of content is accessible:

  • Music, Podcasts, News: Set to “Clean” to filter explicit content
  • Movies: Set an age-appropriate rating (G, PG, PG-13, etc.)
  • TV Shows: Same idea – set the max rating
  • Books: Toggle “Clean” to filter explicit written content
  • Apps: Set the age rating (4+, 9+, 12+, 17+)
  • Web Content: This is critical. Options are:
    • Unrestricted – no filtering
    • Limit Adult Websites – blocks known adult sites and lets you add custom blocked/allowed sites
    • Allowed Websites Only – only sites you specifically approve will load

For younger kids (under 10), “Allowed Websites Only” is the safest option, but it requires you to manually add every website they need. “Limit Adult Websites” is more practical for most families with older kids.

Privacy Settings

Control which apps can access:

  • Location Services
  • Contacts
  • Calendars
  • Photos
  • Microphone
  • Camera

Review each one carefully. There’s no reason a game needs access to your child’s contacts or camera.

Allow Changes

This section prevents your child from changing certain settings:

  • Passcode Changes: Set to “Don’t Allow” (prevents them from removing the Screen Time passcode)
  • Account Changes: Set to “Don’t Allow” (prevents them from modifying their Apple ID)
  • Cellular Data Changes: Your preference
  • Location Sharing: Set to “Don’t Allow” if you use location sharing for safety

Step 6: Set Up Communication Safety

Apple’s Communication Safety features have expanded significantly. Here’s what to enable:

Communication Limits

  1. In Screen Time, tap Communication Limits
  2. During Allowed Screen Time: Choose who your child can communicate with (Everyone, Contacts Only, or specific groups)
  3. During Downtime: Most parents restrict this to “Specific Contacts” – just family members

Sensitive Content Warning

This is Apple’s on-device feature that detects and blurs nudity in photos received through Messages, AirDrop, and other apps.

  1. Go to Settings > Privacy & Security > Sensitive Content Warning
  2. Toggle it on

This works entirely on-device – Apple never sees the photos. It simply blurs the image and gives your child the option to view it or not, along with resources if they need help.

Contact Key Verification

For older teens, this ensures they know they’re actually communicating with who they think they are, protecting against sophisticated impersonation.

Step 7: Set Up Find My

Location sharing isn’t about surveillance – it’s about safety. Here’s how to set it up respectfully:

  1. On your child’s iPhone, go to Settings > [Name] > Find My
  2. Make sure Find My iPhone is on
  3. Enable Share My Location
  4. In the Find My app, make sure your child is sharing their location with you

Healthy approach: Frame this as a two-way street. Share your location with them too. “We all share locations in this family so we know everyone is safe.” That feels very different from “I’m tracking you.”

Step 8: Configure Safari and Browsing

Beyond the content restrictions you already set, there are a few more Safari settings worth adjusting:

  1. Go to Settings > Safari (on your child’s device)
  2. Turn off Allow Cross-Site Tracking (limits ad tracking)
  3. Consider turning on Hide IP Address
  4. In Advanced > Website Data, you can periodically clear stored data

For younger kids, consider installing a kid-friendly browser app as their default and restricting Safari access entirely through Content Restrictions.

Step 9: Manage Notifications

Notifications are an underrated source of stress and distraction for kids. Take a few minutes to clean them up:

  1. Go to Settings > Notifications on your child’s device
  2. For each app, decide whether notifications should be:
    • Off entirely
    • Delivered quietly (no sound or banner)
    • Delivered normally

At minimum, turn off notifications for games and non-essential apps during school hours. You can do this with Scheduled Summary or Focus modes.

Step 10: Review and Adjust Regularly

Setting up parental controls isn’t a one-time event. Your child’s maturity and needs change over time, and the settings should evolve with them.

Monthly: Review Screen Time reports together with your child. Look at which apps they use most, how much total time they spend, and how often they pick up their phone. Use this as a conversation starter, not an interrogation.

Every 6 months: Revisit content restrictions and app limits. A 10-year-old’s settings shouldn’t be the same as when they were 8. Gradually loosening restrictions as your child demonstrates responsibility builds trust and teaches self-regulation.

As needed: When new apps or situations come up, address them. Your kid wants to join Discord? Have a conversation, set up the appropriate safety settings, and decide together.

The Conversation That Makes It All Work

Here’s the thing – parental controls are a safety net, not a cage. The most important part of this whole process isn’t in the settings app. It’s the conversation you have with your child.

Some talking points that work:

  • “These are training wheels, not punishments.” Explain that just like they learned to ride a bike with training wheels, they’re learning to use technology safely. The restrictions will loosen as they get older and demonstrate responsibility.
  • “You can always come to me.” If they see something upsetting, if someone contacts them inappropriately, if they make a mistake – they need to know you’ll help, not punish.
  • “I trust you. I don’t trust the internet.” This frames the controls as protecting them from external threats, not restricting them because you don’t trust them.
  • “Let’s set these up together.” Involving your child in the process gives them ownership and understanding. They’re less likely to try to circumvent rules they helped create.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

“My kid figured out the Screen Time passcode.” Change it. Use something unrelated to birthdays, addresses, or any number they might guess. You can reset it via your Apple ID if needed.

“They’re using a friend’s device to get around restrictions.” This is common and normal. No technical solution replaces ongoing conversation. Talk about why certain content is restricted, not just that it’s restricted.

“Screen Time reports seem inaccurate.” Some apps categorize differently than you’d expect. Check individual app usage, not just categories.

“They need certain apps for school that I’ve restricted.” Add those apps to the “Always Allowed” list in Screen Time so they work even during Downtime or when limits are reached.
